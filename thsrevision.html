Q9Define "tourist" and "excursionist". Give ONE example each.
4 marks★▼
Tourist: A person who travels to destinations outside his/her residence and working place, and stays for at least 24 hours, for the purpose of leisure or business. Example: A UK family staying 5 days in Japan.
Excursionist: A person who temporarily visits a destination and stays for less than 24 hours for the purpose of leisure or business, but NOT for transit. Example: A HK resident day-tripping to Shenzhen without an overnight stay.
Q10State THREE defining factors of tourism.
3 marks★▼
1. Length of Stay — must be less than one year (temporary/short-term).
2. Purpose of Visit — not for an activity remunerated from within the destination (e.g. leisure, business, not working there).
3. Distance / Usual Environment — must travel outside their usual environment (not commuting or routine trips).
Q11A person regularly visits the same nearby shopping mall on weekends. Apply THREE defining factors to determine their status.
6 marks★★★▼
Not a tourist. Factor 1 (Usual Environment): Regular trips to the same nearby shopping mall fall within "usual environment" — routine trips are excluded. Factor 2 (Purpose): Shopping may be acceptable but regular/frequent trips do not qualify. Factor 3 (Length of Stay): If the person returns the same day, they could be an excursionist, not a tourist — likely not even an excursionist if it is within usual environment.

Award 2 marks per factor applied correctly, total 6.

Q11Distinguish between micro and macro factors influencing tourist flows. Give examples of each.
4 marks★★▼
Micro factors = push and pull factors at the individual level. Push: internal motivations — escape, rest, prestige, adventure, social interaction. Pull: destination attractiveness — attractions, climate, lower cost.
Macro factors = broader external forces. Political: visa policies, currency control, political stability. Economic: income levels, exchange rates. Trend: changing tourism patterns (e.g. rise of independent travel).
